Sweden

Wave 1 · Reopening with entity · 11 May 2026

Sweden's registered AED installed base has grown 3× since 2013 — from ~8,000 to ~24,351 — but only one-third (33%) is accessible 24/7. The market is sizeable (~10-20 MEUR/yr), digitally mature (DESI #4), and structurally underserved on the subscription side: no top-3 producer is investing in DTC infrastructure. Density gap — Sweden trails Denmark and the Netherlands

Sweden's 55+ population is expected to grow ~15-20% by 2030 (SCB), with ~6,000-10,000 out-of-hospital cardiac arrests/year today. Despite being an early adopter, Sweden trails on every key deployment metric.

Five structural drivers sustain long-term growth

1
Regulatory momentum
Predictable, time-bound demand events
AFS workplace first-aid obligations, BFHS residential proposal, Socialstyrelsen national action plan. Three regulatory initiatives converging on AED deployment timelines.
2
Digital awareness
'Hjärtstartare' search rising · e-commerce growing
Mainstream media (SVT, Aftonbladet, Expressen) expanded AED coverage materially. Online interest rising; e-commerce now meaningfully larger than 4 years ago. No top-3 producer is investing in DTC — whitespace for digital-first.
3
24/7 accessibility gap
67% locked behind office doors
Only 33% available 24/7. Workplace segment (8,204 units) at just 9.2% 24/7. Outdoor cabinet retrofit market estimated at 24-38 MSEK — near-term opportunity not requiring new AED sales.
4
Demographic ageing
55+ pop +18% by 2030
Cardiac arrest incidence rises with age. Sweden has 2,300 AEDs/M pop vs Denmark's 5,100. Closing the gap requires 15,000-20,000 additional units.
5
National ambitions
Hjärtuppropet: 50K AEDs by 2030
HLR-rådet aims for 1,000 cardiac arrest survivors/year. Hjärtuppropet targets doubling registered AEDs to 50,000 and raising 24/7 share to 50%. National ambitions underpin sustained demand.

Three regulatory initiatives — converging demand events

Socialstyrelsen — National Action Plan for CVD
Commissioned Nov 2025 · Implementation 30 May 2026 · Final 15 Oct 2028
Government tasked Socialstyrelsen + Läkemedelsverket with national action plan covering prevention, early detection, treatment. CVD is SE's #1 cause of death (~30,000/yr, 60bn SEK societal cost). Strengthened OHCA response = direct AED demand uplift.
BFHS — Residential AED Proposal
≥10 dwellings = mandatory AED · LSO 2 kap 2 § amendment
Branschrådet för Hjärtstartare i Sverige proposes that buildings with ≥10 dwellings must have an AED on the same legal footing as fire-safety equipment. Cites DK, FR (2018), IT (2021), NL, JP. Dialogue with all parliamentary parties; no formal bill yet.
Workplace First-Aid Obligations
Risk assessment + "appropriate first-aid equipment"
Employers must risk-assess and supply first-aid equipment proportionate to risks. AEDs not explicitly mandated, but the Swedish Resuscitation Council recommends them — de facto expectation in higher-risk and public-facing workplaces.

Section 02 · Competitive Landscape

The Swedish AED value chain — three layers

AED adoption is well established in Sweden, with multiple suppliers and widespread availability across segments. Customer access is determined by distributor and reseller relationships and channel structure. While many resellers exist, parts of the market are effectively controlled through strong or exclusive partnerships — the key dynamic being Laerdal Medical SE, a major distributor closely tied to Philips.

Key takeaways — Swedish competitive landscape

1
Sizeable and still growing
Swedish AED market worth ~10-20 MEUR/year (excl. hospital/ambulance defibrillators) in device sales. Annual unit growth ~7-8%.
2
Growth in new end-user segments
Traditional buyers (municipalities, healthcare, large corporates) remain the volume base, but the fastest-growing pockets are sports clubs, gyms, schools, SMEs — under-penetrated, increasingly buying online.
3
Two competitor archetypes
Incumbents sell into institutional accounts via own sales reps and framework agreements. Digital challengers (Pulse4all-style) sell online to SMEs and sports clubs with transparent pricing, fast delivery, self-service.
4
New commercial models reshaping economics
Challengers are rolling out mail/parcel distribution, leasing and subscription financing — lowering the upfront hurdle and shifting the P&L from one-off device sales toward recurring service, training and consumables revenue.
